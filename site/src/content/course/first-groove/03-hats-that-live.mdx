A great loop isn't a loop — it's a pattern that never plays quite the same way twice.
Two tools get you there without programming a single extra pattern.

## Probability: the dice inside each step

<PanelHighlight controls={['pattern-settings', 'gate']} caption="Probability hides behind a second press of Pattern Settings; micro steps behind Gate." />

1. Select `Drum 3` (your hats). Press `Pattern Settings` **twice** (or
   `Shift + Pattern Settings`) — **Probability View**.
2. Tap a step; pads 17–24 become an 8-level ladder: 100%, 87.5%, 75%, 62.5%, 50%,
   37.5%, 25%, 12.5% chance that step fires on any given pass.
3. Keep your anchor hats at 100%. Add a few *extra* hats on 16th positions you left
   empty, and set them to 50% or 37.5%. Play. The pattern now improvises within
   rules you set.
4. Two behaviors to know: everything stacked on a step lives or dies **together**,
   and clearing a step (or live-recording onto it) resets it to 100%.

## Micro steps: between the grid lines

Each step secretly subdivides into **6 micro positions**. That's where ratchets
(drum rolls) and off-grid feel live.

5. Press `Gate` while on a drum track, select a hat step, and look at pads 17–22:
   six toggles, one per micro position. The first is the on-beat hit you already
   have. Toggle on positions 3 and 5 — that step now fires three times in one step:
   a ratchet. Put one at the end of a bar for a fill.
6. For feel rather than rolls, there's also **Micro Step View** (`Shift + Gate`),
   where individual notes can sit 1–5 ticks late — you'll lean on it for synths in
   the next project; on drums, the per-step toggles above do the micro work.
8. Also try: record a live hat pass with quantise **off** (`Shift + Record` toggles
   it; while holding `Shift`, drum-track Record shows bright green = quantised, dim
   red = free). Your human timing lands on micro steps and stays human.

<TheoryBridge>
  Probability is a drummer's vocabulary of "sometimes". A live drummer doesn't decide
  in advance which pass gets the extra ghost hit — they roll dice with muscle memory.
  You just gave the sequencer the same dice: anchors at 100%, decorations at 25–50%.
</TheoryBridge>

<JamTip>
  Probability hats are jam insurance: your loop stays interesting during the guitar
  player's third solo chorus without you touching anything.
</JamTip>

<Checkpoint>
  Add 50%-probability decoration hits without wrecking the anchor groove; program a
  three-hit ratchet on one step; record a non-quantised pass and hear the difference.
</Checkpoint>
