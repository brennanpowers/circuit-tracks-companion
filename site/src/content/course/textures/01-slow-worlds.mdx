Everything until now assumed a bar of 16ths at dance tempo. Ambient music breaks
that assumption four different ways, and all four tools live in **Pattern Settings**
and **Gate** — views you already know.

<PanelHighlight controls={['pattern-settings', 'gate']} caption="All four ambient tools hide behind two buttons you already know: Pattern Settings and Gate." />

## Sync rates: tracks at different speeds

1. New project, slow-ish tempo (70–90). Put a simple chord drone idea on Synth 2 and
   a sparse melodic figure on Synth 1.
2. Select Synth 2, press `Pattern Settings`. Everything this lesson needs lives on
   this one screen, on different rows: the top two rows are the pattern's steps
   (start/end points), the **third row of pads (17–24)** is the **sync rate** — how
   fast this track's steps tick relative to the BPM: 1/4, 1/4T, 1/8, 1/8T,
   **1/16 (default, the row's 5th pad)**, 1/16T, 1/32, 1/32T — and the bottom-right
   pads (29–32) are the play order.

3. Set Synth 2 to **1/4**: its 16 steps now span *four bars* — a slow harmonic tide
   under Synth 1's normal-speed sparkle. Rate changes during playback politely wait
   for the end of the current cycle.

## Drones: notes that never re-trigger

4. On your pad track, enter a chord on step 1 and set gate to 16 — it sustains the
   whole pattern but *re-attacks* every pass.
5. The true drone: in Gate View select the step, hop to Micro Step View
   (`Shift + Gate` or press `Gate` again), and toggle **pad 24** — tie-forward. The
   note now holds *through* the loop boundary without re-triggering, indefinitely.
   (Adding a new note to a tied step later? It won't inherit the tie — toggle
   tie-forward off and on to re-capture the whole stack.)

## Odd lengths and directions

6. In Pattern Settings, tap a step pad to move the pattern's **end point**: a
   13-step melody over a 16-step drone drifts out of phase and realigns every 13
   bars — instant evolving music, zero extra notes. (`Shift` + pad moves the start
   point; trimmed steps keep their data, glowing dim red.)
7. Pads 29–32: **play order** — Forward, Reverse, **Ping-pong**, Random. Ping-pong
   turns an arpeggio into a pendulum; Random turns a five-note cell into wind
   chimes. Combine with probability for melodies that never repeat.

<TheoryBridge>
  A 13-against-16 phase pattern is a *tala* trick — additive rhythm, not divisive.
  You know this feeling from riffs in 7 that resolve against a 4/4 drummer. Here the
  device holds both meters perfectly forever, which no human rhythm section will do
  for you at 2am.
</TheoryBridge>
