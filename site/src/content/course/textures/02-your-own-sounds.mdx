The 128 factory patches were someone else's taste. Under them sits a serious
synthesizer — Novation's Nova-family engine (the MiniNova lineage): two oscillators
with 30 waveforms each (virtual-analogue *and* wavetables), noise, ring mod,
multimode filters, three envelopes, two LFOs, and per-patch insert FX. All of it is
editable — just not from the hardware. Plug into **Components** and open the
**Synth Editor**.

## First patch: a slow pad, on purpose

One honesty note before we start: this lesson assumes **Components is open in front
of you**. Novation updates the app's layout from time to time, so instead of "click
here" directions you get the stable concepts — every version of the Synth Editor has
the same building blocks: two oscillators, a filter, three envelopes, two LFOs, and
the macro-mapping section. Find each panel as it comes up. Two definitions you'll
need immediately: an **oscillator** is the raw tone generator (the "string" of the
synth), and a **wavetable** is a scrollable set of wave shapes rather than one fixed
wave — its **Index** control picks your position in the set, and moving it *morphs*
the tone. And the **LFO**: a knob that turns itself, slowly, on a loop — it plays no
notes, it just wiggles whatever you point it at.

At the computer, device connected, pick an *empty-ish* patch slot in the editor's
patch browser (don't overwrite Patch 1 — pick something deep in page 4 you'll never
miss):

1. **Oscillators:** Osc 1 to a warm virtual-analogue wave (saw or triangle); Osc 2 to
   a *wavetable*, detuned a few cents against Osc 1. Sweep the wavetable **Index**
   slowly and listen — that's the sound of the 2020s ambient palette.
2. **Filter:** low-pass, 12 dB/oct for gentle, cutoff modest, a whisper of
   resonance (the whistle-y emphasis right at the cutoff point — Macro 6's job, now
   seen from the inside).
3. **Amp envelope:** attack long (a slow bloom), sustain high, release long enough
   that chord changes cross-fade into each other.
4. **Filter envelope:** slower still, modest depth — the pad brightens as it holds.
   (The engine has a **third envelope**, freely assignable to any parameter — skip
   it today; know it's there for later patches.)
5. **LFO:** slow sine → gentle pitch (a few cents of drift) or wavetable index.
   Motion you feel more than hear.
6. **Insert FX:** the per-patch chorus is the cheapest width money can buy for a pad.

## The part that makes it *playable*

<PanelHighlight controls={['macro-3', 'macro-5', 'macro-7']} caption="Your editor mappings land on real knobs — Macros 3, 5, and 7 become the patch's performance surface." />

7. **Macro mapping** — the editor's best feature: assign up to **4 parameters per
   knob** with independent ranges/depths. Build performance into the patch: Macro 5 →
   cutoff (of course), Macro 7 → LFO depth + wavetable index together (one knob =
   "more alive"), Macro 3 → attack + release together (one knob = "tighter/washier").
8. Save the patch to the device. Unplug. It's now a first-class citizen: playable in
   your key, sequenceable, macro-controllable, sidechain-duckable.

<TheoryBridge>
  Patch design is orchestration. Attack time is bow speed; filter cutoff is how open
  the player's tone is; the LFO is vibrato. You've spent years making these decisions
  with your hands on strings — the editor just asks for them as numbers once, up
  front.
</TheoryBridge>

<JamTip>
  Name patches in Components while you're there — the hardware can't show names, but
  *Components can*, and future-you (browsing patches before a session) will be
  grateful.
</JamTip>
