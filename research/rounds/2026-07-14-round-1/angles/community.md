## Field notes — Circuit Tracks community knowledge (community angle)

### Which tutorials the community actually recommends
The single most-cited resource for Circuit Tracks specifically is **loopop's (Ziv Eliraz) video "Circuit TRACKS vs Circuit OG: Review and full tutorial"** (YouTube s3oJv6KAM0k; loopopmusic.com mirror). It is chapter-marked and doubles as a de-facto manual: sections cover sequencing, sample flip, pattern settings, melodic sequencing, scales, live recording, editing steps, micro steps, probability, mutate, ratchets, automation, MIDI sequencing, effects, sidechain, the pattern grid, view lock, scenes, and playing melodies with samples. Because it explicitly contrasts Tracks against the OG Circuit, it is unusually safe for a beginner trying not to conflate the three devices. Novation's own **"Circuit Tracks — Introduction" video** (YouTube 0To9D9RhlXs) and their official YouTube series are the vendor baseline. A commonly-shared community full walkthrough is **"every feature in the circuit tracks (full tutorial)"** (YouTube GPU7YqxOW6s). Written references people point beginners to: the **official Novation User Guides** (userguides.novationmusic.com — "Learning the basics", "Managing your data", "Using Circuit Tracks' Synths, MIDI Tracks and Drums", "hardware overview"), the **full PDF User Guide** (kraftmusic/focusrite host it), and third-party primers like **Laura's MIDI Heaven** and the **Sweetwater Circuit Series Setup Guide**. The community hub for troubleshooting is **r/novationcircuit** and the long-running **Elektronauts "Novation Circuit Rhythm and Circuit Tracks" thread**. Warning for a beginner: a huge amount of "Circuit" tutorial content is for the 2015 OG or the Rhythm sampler — the layout and features differ, so filter for "Tracks" explicitly.

### The save model — the #1 reported gotcha
The most repeated beginner surprise is how saving works. **Save must be pressed twice.** First press: the Save button flashes and the pad of the last-used Project slot pulses white. Second press: it commits to that same last-used Project slot — i.e. it **overwrites** whatever project you loaded from, silently. To save as a new project instead of overwriting, after the first Save press you must press a **different Project pad** (in Projects view / the slot grid) before the second commit — other pads go dark and the chosen pad flashes green for ~2 seconds. You can **abort a save** after the first press by pressing any other button. There is **no undo and no auto-save**; nothing is written to memory until you deliberately double-press Save, so power-off or a project change loses unsaved edits. Corollary gotcha people hit: **macro-knob movements are not stored unless you record them into automation or Save the project** — twiddling a filter live changes the sound but reverts on reload unless captured. Novation also warns: **do not remove the microSD card during a Save or Load** or you can corrupt/lose data.

### Pattern vs Project vs Scene — the mental-model confusion
Beginners repeatedly conflate these three layers. **Project** = the whole song/session (all 8 tracks, their patterns, patches, mixer, tempo); there are **64 project slots** per pack. **Pattern** = one loop on one track; each of the 8 tracks has **8 pattern slots** (paged, view slots 1–4 then 5–8), default **16 steps**, expandable to **32 steps**. **Pattern chain** = play several of a track's patterns in sequence by holding the first and pressing the last pattern pad — max chain is 8×32 = **256 steps**. **Scene** = a snapshot of the currently-playing set of patterns/chains across all tracks; there are **16 scene pads**, living inside the **Mixer view**, and scenes can themselves be chained into song sections. Practical community complaint: with only 8 patterns/track and 16 scenes, self-contained songs top out around **3–4 minutes** (one owner's record was 3:50), which pushes people toward "loop + live tweak" rather than full arrangement.

### Core step-editing button gestures (no screen, so these are muscle memory)
- **Clear**: hold **Clear** (button lights bright red) then tap a step pad → deletes all notes on that step for the selected track. Hold Clear + a pattern pad clears a pattern; Clear also wipes stored macro automation and projects.
- **Duplicate** (copy/paste): hold **Duplicate** (bright green), tap the **source** step (turns green, its notes turn red), then tap the **destination** step(s). Also duplicates whole patterns to another slot — the standard safety move before destructive edits.
- **View Lock**: hold **Shift** + press the **Patterns/View Lock** button to freeze the top 16 step pads so you can edit one pattern while a different one keeps playing/looping (essential for live editing without the display jumping).
- **Note / Velocity / Gate / Probability views**: dedicated buttons switch the grid so each step's parameters can be entered/edited individually. **Probability is the Shift function of the Pattern Settings button.**
- **Micro-steps**: each of the 16 steps subdivides into **6 micro-steps** for ratchets, strums and triplet/straight feel; with **Record Quantise disabled**, live-played notes land on micro-steps.
- **Change viewed pattern**: hold **Shift** + press a pattern pad.

### Power-user tricks the community shares
- **Mutate** destructively rearranges the current pattern in subtle random ways — always **Duplicate the pattern to an empty slot first** so you keep the original.
- **Probability** per step/note creates evolving variation without extra patterns.
- **Sample flip**: use automation to trigger a **different drum sample on each step**, effectively turning one drum track into a melodic/textural voice — the main workaround for only having 4 drum voices.
- **Melodic use of samples in a DAW**: slice a loop into 8th/16th chunks in your DAW, load them as a pack, and re-sequence on Tracks.
- **Hidden global compressor**: enter **Advanced Setup by holding Shift while powering on**; the comp is a simple on/off (no adjustable parameters) and adds glue for live use.
- **Sidechain**: rhythmic ducking with the trigger source selectable from a drum track; combine with the DJ-style master **Filter** knob for build/drop moves.
- **View Lock + record-enable** lets you overdub one track while the arrangement plays.

### Sample / pack ecosystem and its hard limits
Samples cannot be recorded on Tracks itself (that's the Rhythm) — you load them from a computer/tablet via **Novation Components** (web/desktop; **no iOS Components support** — Android/Chrome/desktop only). All incoming audio is converted to **48 kHz, mono, 16-bit**. Limits (after firmware **1.1.5**): **no single sample longer than 98.3 seconds**, and **196.6 seconds total of sample time per pack**. A **pack** bundles **64 samples + 64 projects + 128 synth patches**. Internal memory holds **one pack**; insert a **microSD card** to hold up to **31 more packs (32 total)**. Where people get content: **Novation Components** (official free packs/patches), **Isotonik Studios** (Circuit Tracks patch/sample store, including free "Big City Dream" 12-preset pack), **SoundGhost**, and **Nu-Trix** free samples. Patch packs install through Components or the third-party Isotonik Circuit Editor.

### Common criticisms / limitations to warn a beginner about
- **No screen** — everything is color/pad memory; scale name, patch name and knob values are invisible ("a memory test"). Biggest adjustment for newcomers.
- **No undo**; the only safety net is Save-before-risky-edit and Duplicate-before-Mutate.
- **Fixed voice allocation**: exactly **2 synths + 4 drums + 2 MIDI**; you can't convert a drum track into a proper melodic sampler, and 4 drum voices feels tight.
- **Drum pitch is a continuous knob** with no note quantization — you can't reliably know which pitch/note you're playing, so pitched/melodic sampling is clumsy.
- **Scales apply globally per project**, not per pattern/track — limits key changes within a song.
- **8 patterns/track** and **16 scenes** cap realistic song length (~3–4 min); arrangements get repetitive.
- **MIDI channel config is global**, not per-project.
- **No multitrack/USB audio export** — to get stems you must solo-cue and record each track through the analog line out into an interface (with attendant noise/timing drift). Line/headphone out only; no digital audio over USB.
- FX are choose-from-a-menu (**16 delay types, 8 reverb types**) with **send-only** control — limited real-time control of time/feedback/depth.
- Nova synth engines are versatile but described as slightly lacking analog "character"/warmth.

### Live-band use (the user's stated goal)
Circuit Tracks is widely regarded as a strong live/jam box. Key live facts: it can **run on internal battery ~4 hours** (charge/power via USB during a set from a phone battery bank). Sync: it can **receive MIDI clock via MIDI In or USB** to lock to a bandmate's gear, or send clock out; the **MIDI Thru port can be repurposed as a second MIDI Out**; there is an **analog sync/pulse out** to drive Volcas/modular. I/O: **two 1/4" balanced mono inputs** (feed an external synth or guitar pedal chain through the internal reverb/delay and mixer), **stereo 1/4" balanced main out**, and a **minijack headphone out** for cueing. The **Mixer view is the live cockpit**: it combines the 16 scene launchers (song-section switching), per-track mutes, levels, and pan — the recommended way to perform sections hands-free. Track color coding on the device: **purple = Synth 1, green = Synth 2, teal = MIDI 1, orange = MIDI 2**, which helps under stage lights. Note: **pattern/scene changes wait for the end of the bar**, so launches stay in time. The two **MIDI tracks** let it sequence/perform external synths, making it a compact brain for a hardware setup on stage. Community caveat for live: macro-based control means you sometimes have to switch views to reach a parameter, which is awkward mid-song — plan and rehearse which knobs you'll grab.
